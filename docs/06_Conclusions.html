Conclusions

In this report, analysis from a set of 45 fecal samples from Leishmaniasis-affected dogs is reported. As indicated in the introduction, these samples correspond to:

  • 10 animals with a 4-timepoint follow-up after receiving anti-leishmaniasis treatment (M1, n=10), probiotic/placebo (M4, n=5+5) and probitic wash-out period(M6, n=5+5).
  • 5 animals with no follow-up that have only been used for baseline analysis (goal 3)

The goals of this study were several and related to the effect of anti-leishmaniasis treatment and probiotic on the gut microbiome of these animals. Importantly, the effective study size (n=10) was very limited and greatly reduced from the initially intended sample size (n=24). Therefore, the statistical power to detect any microbiome alteration was greatly reduced. Larger reduction in statistical power affected the probiotic effect analysis since only n=5 dogs were on the probiotic/placebo group (instead of the n=12 initially intended).

Goal 1: Anti-leishmaniasis treatment

No significant effects on the gut microbiome were detected after leishmaniasis treatment (M1) compared to pre-treatment samples (BL) neither at the taxonomic or functional level. A marginal trend toward the increase of Bifidobacterium genus species was restricted to only 4 of the animals that did not share any feature in a statistically significant way.

Goal 2: Probiotic effect

No significant effects on the gut microbiome were detected after probiotic treatment (M4) compared to pre-probiotic samples (M1) neither at the taxonomic or functional level in the probiotic or placebo groups. A non-significant increasing trend in Blautia in M4 was found for both groups.

Goal 3: Leishmaniasis gut microbiome

The fecal microbiome of leishamiasis affected dogs was rich in Prevotella and Bacteroides species along with Collinsella intestinalis. No association between considered secondary variables and composition or function is found.
